You paid for the software. You implemented it. No one uses it.

Sound familiar?

If you're reading this guide, you've probably lived through one of these scenarios. The new system that seemed perfect in the demo now sits unused while your team quietly reverts to spreadsheets and WhatsApp. Or perhaps you're watching a £30k annual subscription gather digital dust as the office uses it sporadically and site teams ignore it completely. Maybe your implementation was technically flawless but culturally disastrous.

You're not alone. Most software implementations fail, and here's the crucial part: they don't fail because of technical issues. They fail because users simply won't adopt them. The software works perfectly fine. Your team just refuses to use it.

The uncomfortable truth is that buying software is easy. Getting your construction team to actually use it is hard.

### Why Construction Is Different

Generic change management advice doesn't work in construction, and the reason is simple: your workforce isn't sitting in climate-controlled offices watching training videos. They're on site, standing in mud, juggling three projects simultaneously, and working with subcontractors who've been doing things the same way for twenty years.

The challenges are unique to construction. You're managing site managers who barely have time for lunch, let alone an hour-long software training session. You're working with a demographic spread that ranges from tech-savvy graduates to traditional tradespeople who genuinely believe they "aren't good with computers." Your teams are scattered across multiple sites with patchy internet at best. The culture prizes practical results over process improvements. And perhaps most significantly, these are people who've been burned by bad software before and carry deep skepticism about the next "solution."

This isn't resistance to change for the sake of being difficult. This is survival. When someone is managing three sites, chasing supplier delays, and desperately trying to hit this month's targets, a new system that promises to "make things better eventually" doesn't feel like help. It feels like one more burden on an already impossible workload.

## Chapter 1: Why Construction Teams Resist New Software

Before you can drive adoption, you need to understand what you're up against. Resistance isn't personal, it's not laziness, and it's not stupidity. It's rational human behaviour in the face of uncertain change.

Through implementing software across construction companies, we've seen five distinct patterns of resistance emerge. Understanding which one you're dealing with changes how you respond.

### The Five Types of Resistance

#### 1. "I Don't Have Time to Learn This" (Capacity Resistance)

Your site manager is juggling three projects, chasing late deliveries, dealing with a subcontractor dispute, and trying to submit this month's applications. Learning new software feels like one more thing piled onto an already impossible workload.

They're not lying when they say they're too busy. Site managers, PMs, and commercial staff in construction operate at capacity most of the time. There's never a quiet period. "When things calm down" never comes.

The irony is that the system is meant to save them time, but they can't see past the immediate learning curve to the long-term benefit. To them, you're asking someone who's drowning to pause and learn to swim properly.

**How to address it:**
Acknowledge the reality first - they genuinely are slammed. Then show the time savings in concrete terms they can visualize: "You spend 6 hours a week chasing delivery notes - this brings that to zero." Make training bite-sized (20 minutes, not 2 hours) and build it into their workflow rather than adding it as another task.

---

#### 2. "The Old Way Works Fine" (Status Quo Bias)

If you've used Excel for 10 years and delivered projects successfully, why would you believe it's broken? The pain points you see as a leader - no visibility, manual errors, wasted time - aren't visible to them because it's just "how we do things."

They've built workarounds, they know where everything is, they're efficient within the chaos. Your "broken" system is their "normal."

The challenge here is that the time waste is invisible to them. They don't see the 15 hours a week spent on admin because it's distributed across dozens of small tasks. Chasing a delivery note feels like 10 minutes, not the cumulative hours it actually represents.

**How to address it:**
Make the invisible visible. Track their actual time for a week and show them the data. Use their own recent examples - "Remember last month when we paid that invoice twice?" Don't debate the principle, demonstrate the specific pain. Show, don't tell.

---

#### 3. "This Won't Work for Construction" (Skepticism)

Construction has a graveyard of failed software implementations. Generic ERPs that didn't understand construction terminology. Vendors who promised everything and delivered nothing. Systems that worked great in the demo but failed in the real world of muddy sites, patchy internet, and subcontractors who don't use computers.

Anyone who's been in the industry for 10+ years has seen at least one expensive system fail. This isn't unfounded skepticism - it's learned caution. They're protecting themselves from another disappointment.

The deeper issue is trust. They don't trust vendors, they don't trust "solutions," and frankly, they might not fully trust that leadership has chosen wisely this time either.

**How to address it:**
Acknowledge their experience as valid, not as an obstacle. Then prove it with a pilot using real projects, not demos. Let them test it in their world - real deliveries, real invoices, real site conditions. Skepticism evaporates when they see it working in their context. Don't ask them to trust you, show them evidence.

---

#### 4. "I'm Not Tech-Savvy" (Confidence Gap)

Construction culture values practical competence - knowing how to read drawings, manage subcontractors, solve site problems. Struggling with software feels like admitting weakness in front of peers.

A 55-year-old site manager who still uses a paper diary isn't actually incapable of using software. They're afraid of looking incompetent. They're worried about being the person who holds up the training session with "stupid questions." They're concerned that their younger colleagues will judge them.

The confidence gap is especially acute in construction because of the wide demographic spread. You might have a 25-year-old graduate PM comfortable with technology sitting next to someone who's been doing this job successfully for 30 years without needing computers.

**How to address it:**
Normalize the learning curve publicly, but offer private training options for those who don't want to struggle visibly. Use a buddy system pairing tech-confident with tech-hesitant. Choose software with genuinely simple workflows - if core tasks require multiple complex steps, your tech-hesitant users will avoid it. Most importantly, create early wins. Once someone successfully approves their first order from site, confidence builds rapidly.

---

#### 5. "No One Consulted Me" (Ownership Resistance)

This one shows up as silence, not loud complaints. Passive-aggressive non-compliance. People who technically use the system but do the bare minimum and find workarounds whenever possible.

Leadership made a decision that affects their daily work without asking for their input. Even if the software is objectively better, the process felt disrespectful. This is particularly acute with senior staff who expect to be consulted on major operational changes.

The Finance Director saw a demo, approved the budget, announced the change. The people who'll actually use it daily found out via email. They comply outwardly but resist inwardly.

**How to address it:**
Involve actual users in the evaluation and selection process, not just leadership. If you've already bought it without them, acknowledge the oversight directly and ask for their help making it work. Make them co-creators of the configuration - "Help us design the approval matrix for your team." People support what they help create.

---

### The Demographic Challenge in Construction

Beyond these five resistance types, construction has a unique challenge: extreme demographic diversity in one workforce.

**Office staff:**
- Often younger (25-40 years old)
- Digital natives who grew up with smartphones
- Comfortable with technology, expect modern software
- Work in stable environment with good internet

**Site managers:**
- Wide age range (30-65 years old)
- Mix of tech-comfortable and tech-hesitant
- Work in challenging environment (muddy sites, weather, noise)
- Need mobile-first solutions, offline capability

**Operatives and tradespeople:**
- Often older, traditional skill-based workers
- May have limited formal computer experience
- Pride in practical skills, not admin
- Minimal interaction with systems (just need to clock in/out, confirm deliveries)

**The implication:**
One-size-fits-all training doesn't work. You need:
- Different training depth for different roles
- Mobile-first for site teams, desktop for office
- Simplified interfaces for minimal users
- Patience and support for tech-hesitant groups

### The Hidden Cost of Low Adoption (Revisited)

Now that you understand the resistance types, let's revisit what happens when you don't address them.

**Financial costs:**
- £800/month software fee = £9,600/year
- Only 30% adoption = effective cost of £267/user/month (not £20/user)
- Dual systems running (old + new) = reconciliation time, errors, wasted effort
- Lost ROI (time savings and error reduction never materialize)

**Operational costs:**
- Partial data (office uses system, site doesn't) = bad decisions based on incomplete information
- Delays when site teams bypass system (phone calls, WhatsApp, email chains)
- Errors from manual re-entry between systems
- "Where is this order?" questions that the system should answer automatically

**Cultural costs:**
- Cynicism: "Another software project that didn't work"
- Learned helplessness: "Leadership doesn't understand our reality"
- Good staff frustration: Your best people hate inefficiency and may leave
- Future resistance: Next time you try to implement something, resistance will be even higher

**The compounding effect:**
Low adoption doesn't just waste the current investment - it makes future change harder. Each failed implementation builds more skepticism, more resistance, more "I told you it wouldn't work."

## Chapter 2: The Five Pillars of Successful Adoption

Understanding resistance is step one. Overcoming it requires a systematic approach.

Through implementing software across construction companies, we've found that five pillars consistently emerge as critical to success. Miss any one of them and your chances of achieving meaningful adoption drop significantly. These pillars build on each other sequentially, which means you cannot skip ahead to Pillar 4 without doing the groundwork in Pillars 1 through 3.

---

### Pillar 1: Get Buy-In Before You Buy

The most expensive mistake happens before you even start implementation: buying software without involving the people who will actually use it.

Here is a scenario we see repeatedly. The Finance Director watches a great demo, approves the budget, signs the contract, and announces it via company email. The first time users hear about the new system is when they are told that training is mandatory next Tuesday. You have just created ownership resistance before anyone has even logged in.

#### Form an Evaluation Team That Includes Actual Users

Your evaluation team should not consist solely of leadership. Include office staff from finance, admin, and commercial roles. Include site managers and project managers who will use the system daily. Deliberately include at least one sceptic who will ask hard questions during demos, and include someone who is tech-hesitant because if they find the software easy to use, everyone else will too. The people who will use the system daily need a voice in the selection process.

#### Run Real Demos Using Your Actual Workflows

Generic demos do not work for construction. When you evaluate software, use your real scenarios. Ask the vendor to show you how you would process the delivery that arrived yesterday. Ask them to walk you through creating a purchase order for materials on a specific project you are currently running. Ask how someone would approve an urgent order from site. If the software cannot handle your real workflows, you need to find out now rather than after you have signed the contract.

#### Pilot with Multiple Vendors Using Real Work

Do not just watch demos and make a decision. Run short pilots lasting one to two weeks with two or three vendors using actual work. Use real projects, real suppliers, and real orders during these pilots. Let your team test drive each option and collect honest feedback by asking them directly whether they would use this system daily. The software that looks best in a sales demo is often not the one that works best in practice.

#### Make the Selection Decision Together

Do not simply announce the decision to your team. Instead, involve them in making it. Present the options and say that you tested three systems and here is what you found, then ask what they think. Let team members advocate for their preferred choice. Even if you ultimately override their preference, they have been heard and their input has been considered. This matters enormously for adoption.

People support what they help create. When the team says "we chose this" instead of "management chose this," you have already won half the adoption battle.

#### Identify Your Champions Early

During the evaluation process, watch for natural patterns that reveal who your champions should be.

Your software champions should be one per department or team. Look for the people who ask the best questions during demos, who get excited about the possibilities, who have credibility and respect within their team, and who others naturally turn to for guidance. These are your champions, and they will drive the change from within their teams.

Your tech-friendly super-users should number two or three per team. Look for people who pick up the software quickly during pilots, who naturally help others when they get stuck, who are generally comfortable with technology, and who enjoy problem-solving. These super-users will become embedded trainers within each team.

You need both champions and super-users because they serve different purposes. Champions provide leadership and vision for the change. Super-users provide hands-on support and training. A single champion cannot train 20 people single-handedly without burning out, but a champion supported by two or three super-users can effectively support a team through the entire learning curve.

Do not wait until after purchase to find your champions. Identify them during evaluation and make them part of the decision from the beginning.

### Pillar 2: Win Hearts and Minds

Most companies make the same communication mistake. They send an email saying they are switching to a new system on a specific date and that training is mandatory. This approach gets compliance because people show up to training, but it does not generate buy-in because people do not actually use the system afterwards.

The better approach is to make your team want to use the new software by showing them how it solves their specific daily problems.

#### Document Their Real Frustrations First

Before you announce the change, talk to your team and find their pain points. Ask what annoys them about the current system, what makes them groan every Monday morning, what causes them to stay late every week, and what manual task they complain about most.

Get specific examples from specific people. Ask site managers what their biggest delivery tracking frustration is. Ask your finance team what part of invoice processing drives them crazy. Ask your project managers what approval delay cost them the most time last month. Write these frustrations down because you will use them in all your communication about the change.

#### Connect the New Tool to Their Problems

Do not lead your communication with company benefits. Lead with personal benefits instead.

Company-focused messaging like "better visibility for management" or "improved data accuracy" or "enhanced reporting capabilities" does not resonate with users. User-focused messaging works better, such as "no more chasing signatures because you can approve orders from your phone on site" or "stop re-entering supplier details fifty times because it is stored once and used everywhere" or "get delivery photos automatically so there are no more arguments about proof."

Even better is messaging that uses their actual words. Use the frustrations you documented earlier. Tell them that you remember last month when they spent three hours chasing that missing delivery note and that this system fixes that problem. Tell them that they said they hate emailing purchase orders back and forth for approvals and that now it routes automatically. Remind them about the duplicate invoice you paid in January and explain that the system would have caught it.

When you use their own examples and their own language, they cannot argue with it because you are literally solving the problems they told you about.

#### Make It Personal, Not Procedural

Translate every company benefit into a personal benefit. Instead of saying the new system will improve operational efficiency, tell them they will spend thirty minutes less per day on admin paperwork. Instead of saying you need better procurement visibility, tell them they will know exactly where their order is without making phone calls. Instead of saying this supports your digital transformation strategy, tell them they can approve urgent purchase orders from site instead of driving back to the office.

#### Why Hearts and Minds Matters for Adoption

Here is what happens when users hit the inevitable learning curve in the first two weeks. Everything is new and confusing. They cannot find things, they do not know how to do what they used to do in Excel, and tasks take longer than the old way. This is the danger zone. If they do not believe the upside is worth the frustration, they will revert to the old way the moment things get difficult.

When you have won hearts and minds, users think differently. They tell themselves that this is frustrating right now, but they remember how much they hated chasing delivery notes, so they will push through because they know it will be better once they learn it. Without hearts and minds, users think this is frustrating and they do not see the point, so they go back to Excel.

The difference is belief. Belief that the pain is temporary and the benefit is real.

#### Communication Cadence

Four weeks before launch, announce the change. Explain the frustrations you are solving using their examples. Share the evaluation process and explain what you tested and why you chose this system. Set expectations clearly by acknowledging that yes, there will be a learning curve, and here is the support you are providing.

Two weeks before launch, run demos using their workflows rather than generic examples. Answer questions and concerns. Share the training schedule. Introduce the champions and super-users as the people who will support them.

One week before launch, send a final reminder of training dates. Share quick-start resources like videos and cheat sheets. Build anticipation by reminding them that you are fixing that delivery note nightmare next week.

On launch day, celebrate it rather than apologising for it. Use positive framing by telling them that today you solve the problems they have been telling you about. Make sure champions are visible and available. Make the first day a success even if it is messy.

Ongoing for the first month, share progress weekly with factual updates like the number of purchase orders approved with zero missing signatures. Address struggles directly by acknowledging that you know the supplier selection screen is confusing and sharing a two-minute video showing the shortcut. Reinforce the why by reminding them that you used to chase delivery notes for hours and this week there were none.

### Pillar 3: Make Training Practical, Not Theoretical

Most companies make the same training mistake. They run a two-hour classroom session covering all features using generic scenarios, delivered lecture-style to mixed groups of people with different roles. The result is that users are overwhelmed, forget eighty percent of what they learned within a week, and do not know how to apply any of it to their actual work.

The better approach is role-based, hands-on training using their real workflows.

#### Segment Training by Role

Different roles need different training depth because they use different features with different frequency.

Site managers need about thirty minutes of mobile-focused training. Cover mobile approvals because that is their primary use case, delivery photo uploads, quick order status checks, and who to contact for emergency support. Do not waste their time on features they will never use.

Office-based project managers need about forty-five minutes focused on request creation. Cover creating purchase requests, adding suppliers and comparing quotes, attaching specifications, tracking order status, and handling queries.

Finance teams need about an hour focused on invoice processing. Cover the invoice matching process, handling discrepancies, three-way matching, reporting and exports, and the payment approval workflow.

Procurement and commercial teams need about an hour covering the full system. Cover supplier management, request for quotation workflows, approval configuration, analytics and reporting, and system administration basics.

Match training depth to usage frequency. Site managers who will use three features do not need training on all thirty.

#### Use Real Scenarios, Not Generic Examples

Do not train by saying "click here to create a purchase order, click here to add a line item, click here to submit for approval." This is memorisation, not learning, and users will forget it immediately.

Instead, give them a real task. Tell them they need to order five hundred bricks for the Riverside project and work through it together right now. Then walk through the actual process with their actual suppliers, their actual project codes, and their actual approval chain.

Real scenarios work because people learn by doing, not watching. They see how the software applies to their actual work. They build muscle memory with real data. They can ask contextual questions like "what if the supplier does not have this product?" that would never come up in a generic demo.

#### Hands-On Training, Not Lecture Style

Bad training looks like a trainer demonstrating on a projector for two hours while attendees watch passively. Good training looks like everyone having a computer or tablet in front of them. The trainer demonstrates step one, everyone completes step one, the trainer checks that everyone finished it, and then everyone moves to step two together.

Use the "I do, we do, you do" approach. First, the trainer demonstrates the task. Then, everyone does it together with the trainer guiding them through. Finally, each person does it independently while the trainer observes and helps anyone who gets stuck.

#### Provide Job Aids, Not Just Training

People forget training within days. They do not forget a laminated quick-reference card stuck to their desk.

Create quick reference cards for each role. These should be one page, laminated, with step-by-step instructions for their top three workflows. Include screenshots with arrows pointing to the relevant buttons. Include contact information for support at the bottom so they know who to call when they get stuck.

Post these cards everywhere: site offices, desks, filing cabinets using magnet versions, and breakrooms. Make them impossible to avoid.

Create short video tutorials lasting two to three minutes each for specific tasks. Create one for how to approve a purchase order on mobile, one for how to upload a delivery photo, one for how to create a materials request. These are not comprehensive training videos. They are micro-tutorials for specific tasks that users can reference when they get stuck.

Record screen recordings with voiceover showing someone doing each task with a verbal explanation. Post these on your shared drive or intranet where users can access them at any time.

Users will reference these materials constantly during the first two weeks. Budget for creating them properly.

#### Follow-Up Support, Not One-and-Done

The problem with one-time training is that users forget eighty percent of what they learned within a week. When they hit a problem on Thursday, they do not remember what you said in Monday's training session.

The solution is layered support that continues well after the initial training.

During the first week, offer daily office hours. Have your champions and super-users available from nine to ten in the morning and three to four in the afternoon every day. This should be drop-in support with no appointment needed. Users can come in and say they are stuck on something and ask for help.

During weeks two through four, run scheduled drop-in sessions. These should be thirty-minute sessions twice a week where anyone can come with questions. Use these as open forums and as refreshers on common struggles that have emerged.

On an ongoing basis, designate super-users in every team. Every team should have two or three super-users as the first point of contact for questions. Tell users to ask Sarah or John before they call IT. Peer support scales better than a centralised helpdesk and is more accessible for people who feel embarrassed asking for help.

Layered support works because people learn through repetition and reinforcement, not one-time exposure. Budget for ongoing support, not just initial training.

### Pillar 4: Start Small, Control the Blast Radius

Most companies make the same rollout mistake. They announce that everyone switches to the new system on Monday. This is called a big bang rollout, and it almost always causes problems.

When you roll out to everyone simultaneously, if something breaks, chaos spreads company-wide. If there is a workflow problem, one hundred people are affected instead of ten. If resistance emerges, it becomes cultural before you can address it because everyone is struggling at the same time.

The better approach is to pilot with a small group first, prove the system works, fix what does not work, and then roll out team by team in a controlled sequence.

#### Phase 1: Small Pilot Group

The first phase should last two to four weeks with a small pilot group of ten to fifteen people maximum. Select one or two projects or teams. Include a mix of champions, super-users, and representative users from different roles. Deliberately include at least one sceptic because you need their honest feedback to identify real problems.

Small groups matter for several important reasons.

First, they provide a controlled blast radius. If something breaks, only ten people are affected rather than one hundred. It is much easier to provide intensive support to a small group. Problems do not spread company-wide and become cultural catastrophes.

Second, small groups enable faster issue detection. Patterns emerge quickly when you are watching a small group closely. If three people struggle with supplier selection, that is a clear signal that something needs fixing. You can fix problems before the wider rollout rather than discovering them when one hundred people are already frustrated.

Third, small groups contain negativity. If someone resists, their resistance does not spread to the entire company. You can address resistance one-on-one without public drama.

Fourth, small groups allow a higher support ratio. Two or three champions supporting ten users means lots of help per person. This creates a better experience, faster learning, and higher confidence.

Run real work through the system during the pilot. Do not use fake data or practice projects. Use actual suppliers, actual orders, and actual approvals. If the system works in the pilot with real work, it will work in the rollout.

Conduct daily check-ins with your pilot team. At the end of each day, ask what worked, what did not work, and what was confusing. Apply quick fixes for easy problems immediately. Document harder issues for the refinement phase. Celebrate small wins by sharing that you processed twelve purchase orders today with zero errors.

Watch for early resisters during the pilot. Notice who is not engaging with the system. Notice who is finding workarounds to avoid using it. Notice who is complaining publicly. Address resistance immediately. Do not let it spread.

#### Phase 2: Refinement

After the pilot, pause for one to two weeks before the wider rollout. This refinement phase is essential.

Fix the issues you identified during the pilot. If the supplier selection dropdown is confusing, simplify it or add a search function. If the mobile approval button is too small, increase the touch target size. If email notifications are overwhelming, add controls to reduce frequency.

Update your training materials based on what you learned. Common questions from the pilot become your FAQ. Confusing steps get additional screenshots. Create new quick-reference cards for shortcuts that users discovered.

Prepare success stories from the pilot to use in the wider rollout. Collect concrete examples like the Riverside project processing thirty purchase orders in two weeks with zero approval delays, or a site manager approving an urgent order from site in forty-five seconds. Get quotes from pilot users saying things like "once I learned it, this is so much faster than Excel." These stories become your ammunition for convincing the next wave of users.

#### Phase 3: Team-by-Team Rollout

Do not roll out to everyone at once. Go team by team over four to six weeks. In week one, roll out to Team A with the next ten to fifteen people. In week two, roll out to Team B. In week three, roll out to Team C. Continue this pattern until everyone is onboarded.

Team-by-team rollout beats big bang rollout for several reasons. The blast radius stays controlled throughout the process. Champions can focus their support on one team at a time rather than being spread thin across the entire company. Early teams become advocates for later teams because they can share their positive experiences. Issues discovered in Team A get fixed before Team B starts, so each subsequent rollout is smoother than the last.

Your pilot champions become trainers for the rollout. Sarah from the pilot team trains Team A. John from the pilot team trains Team B. Real users training real users is far more credible than an external trainer who has never used the system in a construction context.

Use early wins to build momentum. Share updates like "Team A processed fifty purchase orders last week and here is what they learned." Have pilot users share tips about shortcuts they discovered. Success breeds success.

#### Phase 4: Sunset the Old System

Eventually, you need to kill the old way completely. As long as the old system remains available, some people will continue using it.

Set a hard cutoff date. Announce that there will be no more Excel purchase orders after June first and that everything goes through the new system from that date forward.

Communicate the cutoff date early and repeatedly. Eight weeks before the cutoff, announce that June first is the date. Four weeks before, remind everyone that there are four weeks until you sunset Excel purchase orders. Two weeks before, tell them this is the final two weeks for Excel and they should migrate their workflows now. One week before, remind them it is the last week of Excel purchase orders. On cutoff day, make the old system view-only or shut it down completely.

A hard cutoff is non-negotiable for several reasons. As long as the old way is available, some people will use it. Dual systems create reconciliation hell where you are constantly trying to match data between two places. Your ROI never materialises if only sixty percent of users adopt the new system. Most importantly, keeping the old system running sends a cultural message that the new system is optional rather than how you work now.

Provide support for stragglers who resist until the last minute. Some people will not switch until they absolutely have to. Do not shame them for this. Help them instead. Approach them directly and offer to show them how to do their tasks in the new system.

### Pillar 5: Track Progress and Address Resistance

Most companies make the same measurement mistake. They launch the software, hope it sticks, never measure adoption, and wonder why it failed six months later.

The better approach is to track leading indicators weekly, share progress factually with the team, and address non-adoption directly when you spot it.

#### Track Leading Indicators Weekly

Leading indicators tell you whether adoption is happening in real time. Track these weekly for at least the first three months.

Track active users by counting how many people logged in each week. Your target should be eighty-five percent or higher by week eight. If you are not on track for that target, you need to intervene.

Track system usage versus the old way by measuring the percentage of purchase orders created in the new system versus Excel. Your target should be ninety percent or higher by week twelve.

Track mobile engagement if your system has mobile capabilities. Count the number of mobile approvals from site. Your target should be sixty percent or higher of site-based approvals happening via mobile by week eight.

Track support tickets by counting the number of "how do I do this?" questions. This number should be high in weeks one and two as people learn the system, then decline sharply by week six as people become comfortable.

Track key transactions including purchase orders created, approvals completed, and invoices processed. Compare these numbers to your pre-implementation baseline to see whether volume is maintaining or improving.

Do not wait for monthly reports. Check these metrics weekly so you can intervene quickly if adoption stalls.

#### Track Lagging Indicators Monthly

Lagging indicators tell you whether you are getting the return on investment you were promised. Track these monthly.

Track time savings by measuring how long invoice processing takes now versus before implementation. Your target should be the time reduction your business case promised, typically around twenty percent.

Track error reduction by measuring your invoice matching error rate and counting duplicate payments caught. Your target should be ninety percent fewer errors than before.

Track process speed by measuring approval turnaround time and order-to-delivery cycle time.

Track cost savings including admin time saved, better supplier pricing if applicable, and rush order premiums avoided.

Use these lagging indicators for business case validation. You promised fifty thousand pounds in annual savings, so use these metrics to prove you are delivering it.

#### Share Progress and Recognise Effort

Track the metrics and share them with the team, but keep your communication factual rather than theatrical. Construction teams can smell fake enthusiasm from a mile away.

Effective progress updates sound like this: "We processed forty-five purchase orders this week, up from thirty-two last week." Or: "Finance team matched ninety-seven percent of invoices first time this week." Or: "Mobile approvals are now sixty percent of total approvals, which shows good uptake from site teams."

Avoid overly enthusiastic celebration emails with excessive exclamation marks. Avoid forced public recognition that makes people uncomfortable. Avoid patronising language like "well done team" with multiple exclamation marks. Your team are professionals, not children.

If your company already has a culture of public recognition, continue it during the rollout. If you do not have that culture, do not introduce it during a software rollout because it will feel fake and manipulative.

Quiet recognition matters more than public fanfare. A one-on-one conversation telling a champion that the system would not have worked without their help is worth more than a company-wide email. Private thank-yous to super-users who have trained their peers have more impact than public announcements. Direct acknowledgment that someone's effort made a difference is what people actually value.

Share metrics to show progress, not to manufacture enthusiasm. The data speaks for itself.

#### Address Non-Adoption Directly

Data does not lie. If someone has not logged in for two weeks, they are not using the system. Do not ignore this. Address it directly.

Have a private one-on-one conversation. Tell them you noticed they have not been using the software and ask what is blocking them. Be curious and supportive, not accusatory.

When they say they are too busy, acknowledge that you understand and suggest doing a ten-minute session right now with their actual work. Offer to sit with them and walk through it together.

When they say they do not understand it, tell them that is completely normal because the software is new. Offer to pair them with Sarah or another successful user who can help them.

When they say the old way is faster for them, acknowledge that right now it probably is because they are expert in Excel. Suggest tracking their time for a week because you think they will be surprised how the new way compares once they are comfortable with it.

When they say it does not work for their workflow, ask them to show you their workflow. Work together to see if you can configure the system to work for them, or identify whether there is a legitimate gap that needs addressing.

Provide extra support rather than punishment. Offer additional training. Pair them with a super-user. Create a simplified quick-start guide for their specific tasks.

Set clear expectations at the end of the conversation. Tell them you are committed to helping them succeed with this system, that using it is not optional because this is how you work now, and ask what support they need to get there.

#### Hold Leaders Accountable

If leadership bypasses the system, everyone else will too. When the Managing Director still approves via email, the team thinks the system is optional. When a Director still uses Excel for their purchase orders, the team follows their example. When champions do not use the system, they have zero credibility when asking others to use it.

Leadership must be visible users of the system. The Managing Director approves only via the system with no email exceptions. Directors create their purchase orders in the system. Champions use it daily and share their experience with others.

Lead by example or adoption will fail.

## Chapter 3: Common Pitfalls (And How to Avoid Them)

You now have the five-pillar framework. But even with good strategy, there are common mistakes that can derail adoption. Here are the eight pitfalls we see repeatedly, and how to avoid them.

### Pitfall 1: No Executive Sponsorship (And the Danger of Micro-Frustrations)

If the boss doesn't use it, why should anyone else? Teams take cultural cues from leadership. When they see senior people bypass the system, the message is clear: "This isn't really important."

But it's not just about blatant bypassing. The more insidious problem is micro-frustrations from senior staff.

A director sighs heavily when logging in. A senior manager mutters "this is so slow" under their breath. A QS makes a small joke in a meeting about "the new system we all love." Individually, these seem harmless. Collectively, they're permission for everyone else to be openly frustrated.

When senior staff express negativity - even small, casual negativity - it gives the team cultural permission to resist. If the Commercial Director is publicly frustrated, site managers think "well, if even they don't like it, why should I bother?"

**The standard required of leadership:**
Senior staff must behave neutrally or positively about the system, even during the difficult learning curve period. Not fake enthusiasm, but professional neutrality. Save the frustrations for private conversations with the implementation team, not public displays in front of the people you're asking to adopt.

This doesn't mean leadership can't give feedback or raise issues. It means they do it constructively in the right forums, not through theatrical sighs and eye rolls that spread cultural resistance.

**How to avoid it:**
- MD/Director must be visible, active users
- No email approvals if the system requires in-app approvals
- No micro-frustrations in team settings (sighs, muttering, jokes at the system's expense)
- Leadership models the behaviour they want to see
- Private feedback channels for legitimate issues

---

### Pitfall 2: Poor Timing

**What it looks like:**
- Launching during year-end closeout
- Rolling out in the busiest project delivery month
- Training scheduled during month-end when finance is slammed

**Why it kills adoption:**
"I don't have time for this" becomes a legitimate complaint, not resistance. When people genuinely are drowning, adding a new system feels cruel, not helpful.

**How to avoid it:**
- Launch during quieter periods (if such a thing exists in construction)
- Avoid month-end, quarter-end, year-end
- Check project delivery schedules
- Ask team: "When would be least disruptive?"

Sometimes there's no perfect time, but there are definitely wrong times.

---

### Pitfall 3: Assuming "Intuitive" Means "No Training Needed"

**What it looks like:**
- "The software is really easy, they'll figure it out"
- 30-minute overview instead of hands-on training
- No job aids or reference materials

**Why it kills adoption:**
Even genuinely intuitive software has a learning curve. "Figuring it out" means clicking around randomly, getting frustrated, and giving up.

**How to avoid it:**
- Assume everyone needs structured onboarding, even if the UI is simple
- Hands-on training with real scenarios
- Quick reference guides for common tasks
- Available support when they get stuck

"Intuitive" is marketing speak. "Learned quickly with good training" is reality.

---

### Pitfall 4: Ignoring the Mobile Experience

**What it looks like:**
- Software works great on desktop, terrible on mobile
- Site teams expected to use tablets that don't work offline
- Mobile features exist but weren't explained in training

**Why it kills adoption:**
Office adopts (desktop works fine), site teams don't (mobile is unusable). You get 50% adoption and partial data, which means bad decisions.

**How to avoid it:**
- Test mobile experience before you buy
- Train site teams specifically on mobile workflows
- Ensure offline capability for areas with poor connectivity
- Make mobile approval stupidly simple (big buttons, minimal clicks)

Construction is a mobile industry. If site teams can't use it, you've failed.

---

### Pitfall 5: No Consequences for Non-Compliance

**What it looks like:**
- Old system still works, so people keep using it
- "We'd prefer you use [software], but if you're more comfortable with Excel..."
- No hard cutoff date
- Dual systems running indefinitely

**Why it kills adoption:**
If the old way is available, some people will use it forever. And if some people use it, others will too. Before long, you're back to Excel and the new system is abandoned.

**How to avoid it:**
- Hard cutoff date, communicated clearly and repeatedly
- Old system becomes view-only or shut down completely
- No email approvals if system requires in-app approvals
- "This is not optional - this is how we work now"

Harsh? Maybe. Necessary? Absolutely.

---

### Pitfall 6: Forgetting "What's In It For Me?"

**What it looks like:**
- Messaging focused on company benefits: "Better visibility for management"
- No connection to individual pain points
- "We need to do this for compliance/reporting/strategy"

**Why it kills adoption:**
Users don't care about company-level metrics. They care about their daily work. If you can't explain how it helps them personally, they won't engage.

**How to avoid it:**
- Document individual frustrations
- Show how the tool solves their problems
- Personal benefits first, company benefits second
- Use their language and their examples

"This makes your life easier" beats "This supports our digital strategy" every time.

---

### Pitfall 7: One-and-Done Training

**What it looks like:**
- 2-hour training session on Day 1, nothing after
- "If you have questions, email IT"
- No follow-up, no refreshers, no reinforcement

**Why it kills adoption:**
People forget 80% of training within a week. When they hit a problem on Thursday, they don't remember Monday's training. With no support, they give up and revert to the old way.

**How to avoid it:**
- Ongoing support, not one-time training
- Daily office hours (week 1-2)
- Drop-in sessions (week 3-4)
- Embedded super-users in each team as first point of contact
- Quick reference materials always available

Learning happens through repetition and reinforcement, not one exposure.

---

### Pitfall 8: Tolerating Vocal Resisters

**What it looks like:**
- One person publicly complains: "This is rubbish, the old way was better"
- Management ignores it or handles it privately
- Negativity spreads across the team
- Others think it's acceptable to not use it

**Why it kills adoption:**
One vocal opponent can undo months of change management work. When others see resistance being tolerated, they think "maybe I don't have to use it either." Resistance becomes culturally acceptable.

**This is the hard one. Here's the reality:**

**Catch it early:**
If someone is vocally negative about the system, address it immediately. Not next week, not in their next review. Immediately.

**One-on-one conversation:**
"I've noticed you're frustrated with [software]. What's the real issue? How can we help you succeed?"

Approach with curiosity, not accusation. Most resistance has a root cause:
- They don't understand how to use it (training issue)
- It doesn't work for their specific workflow (configuration issue)
- They weren't consulted and feel disrespected (ownership issue)
- They're genuinely tech-hesitant and afraid to fail (confidence issue)

**Provide intensive support:**
- Extra one-on-one training
- Pair with a successful user
- Simplify their workflows
- Daily check-ins for a week

Give them every chance to succeed.

---

**But sometimes, you can't change the person.**

After intensive support, honest conversation, and genuine effort to help... some people will not adopt.

More importantly, some people will actively undermine the rollout with public negativity.

In one implementation, a senior site manager refused to use the system. Loudly complained in team meetings. Encouraged others to stick with Excel. Said "this will never work" repeatedly.

After three weeks of support - one-on-one training, pairing with successful site managers, simplified workflows, direct conversations about behaviour - no change. Still refusing to use it, still bad-mouthing it publicly.

The decision was made to move him to a different role where system use wasn't required.

The result? His negativity stopped spreading. Team adoption jumped from 60% to 95% within 2 weeks. Other users felt leadership was serious about the change. Cultural message sent: "We'll support you, but public undermining won't be tolerated."

---

**The nuclear option: If you can't change the person, change the person.**

This is the LAST resort, not the first. But it's a resort that must be available.

**When to consider it:**
- After genuine effort to support and coach
- When public negativity is spreading
- When their resistance is damaging team morale
- When they're making others think non-compliance is acceptable

**Why it matters:**
Your £50k software investment is at stake. Your team's productivity is at stake. Cultural change is at stake.

One person cannot be allowed to kill a company-wide initiative that benefits everyone else.

**Important caveats:**
- This is removal from role or project, not necessarily termination
- Must be handled through proper HR process
- Must have documented attempts to support them
- Should be rare (if you're doing this frequently, your change management is the problem)

**The cultural signal:**
When you address vocal resistance decisively, everyone else sees:
- Leadership is serious about this
- Support is available, but undermining isn't tolerated
- The change is real, not optional

Removing one toxic resister often unlocks adoption across the entire team.

**Key insight:** Compassion and accountability aren't opposites. You can genuinely try to help someone succeed AND decisively act when they actively undermine the team.

---

### Pitfall Summary

| Pitfall | Warning Sign | Solution |
|---------|--------------|----------|
| No executive sponsorship | Leadership bypasses system or shows micro-frustrations | MD/Directors must be visible users with neutral/positive behaviour |
| Poor timing | Launching during year-end | Avoid peak periods, ask team when |
| Assuming intuitive = easy | No training provided | Structured onboarding for everyone |
| Ignoring mobile | Site teams don't adopt | Test and train mobile workflows |
| No consequences | Dual systems forever | Hard cutoff date, shut down old system |
| Forgetting WIIFM | Generic company benefits | Personal benefits, their language |
| One-and-done training | Users forgotten after Day 1 | Ongoing support, embedded champions |
| Tolerating vocal resisters | Public negativity spreading | Address immediately, support intensively, act decisively if needed |

## Chapter 4: The Complete Adoption Checklist

Theory is helpful. Checklists are better.

This chapter gives you a practical, printable checklist covering everything from preparation through full adoption. Unlike a rigid timeline, this checklist lets you move at your own pace while ensuring you don't skip critical steps.

**How to use this checklist:**
- Print it and post it in your office
- Tick items off as you complete them
- Don't skip ahead - each phase builds on the previous one
- Share it with your implementation team
- Review weekly: "What did we complete? What's next?"

---

### Why a Checklist (Not a Timeline)

**Timelines assume everyone moves at the same speed:**
- Small company (15 people): Might complete pilot in 1 week
- Large company (100 people): Might need 4 weeks for pilot

**Checklists are flexible:**
- Complete Phase 1 in 1 week or 3 weeks - both are fine
- What matters is completing each item thoroughly, not how fast

**Checklists prevent skipping steps:**
- Easy to see what's left
- Hard to rationalize "we'll skip the pilot"
- Clear progress tracking

---

### The Four-Phase Adoption Checklist

### Phase 1: Preparation (Before Launch)

**Goal:** Get ready to launch with champions identified, buy-in secured, and training prepared.

**Implementation Team & Champions:**
- [ ] Form implementation team (mix of enthusiasts and skeptics across departments)
- [ ] **Identify 1 champion per department/team** (enthusiastic, credible, respected)
- [ ] **Find 2-3 "tech-friendly" super-users per team** (quick learners who'll train peers)
- [ ] Involve champions in system configuration decisions

**Software Selection & Configuration:**
- [ ] Involve end-users in software evaluation (form selection team with actual users)
- [ ] Run real demos with your actual workflows (not generic scenarios)
- [ ] Pilot 2-3 vendors if possible (test with real data, real projects)
- [ ] Make selection decision together (team input, not just leadership decree)
- [ ] Configure system settings, approval workflows, permissions
- [ ] Set up project codes, cost codes, supplier categories
- [ ] Create user accounts for pilot group

**Understanding Their Problems:**
- [ ] **Interview teams about current system frustrations** (get specific examples)
- [ ] **Document their pain points in their own words** (you'll use these in messaging)
- [ ] Map current workflows (understand the "as-is" state)
- [ ] Identify which workflows will change most

**Pilot Planning:**
- [ ] Select pilot projects/teams (**keep small: 10-15 people max**)
- [ ] Choose mix of champions, super-users, and representative users
- [ ] Include at least one skeptic (you need honest feedback)
- [ ] Set realistic pilot timeline (2-4 weeks typically)

**Communication & Training Prep:**
- [ ] Draft announcement email **focused on their frustrations** (not company benefits)
- [ ] Announce the change to whole company (explain the "why")
- [ ] **Show how this solves THEIR daily problems** (use their examples and language)
- [ ] Set expectations: "Yes, learning curve. Here's the support we're providing."
- [ ] Create role-specific training materials (site managers need different content than finance)
- [ ] Record 2-3 minute video walkthroughs (task-specific, not comprehensive)
- [ ] Design and print quick reference cards (laminated, one per role)
- [ ] Schedule training sessions (hands-on, not lecture-style)

**Mobile Setup (if applicable):**
- [ ] Test mobile experience thoroughly (site conditions, offline mode)
- [ ] Configure mobile approvals
- [ ] Test mobile workflows with pilot champions

**Final Prep:**
- [ ] Set pilot start date
- [ ] Brief champions on their role (lead by example, support their teams)
- [ ] Brief super-users on their role (embedded trainers, first point of contact)
- [ ] Establish daily check-in schedule for pilot phase

**Phase 1 Complete When:** Pilot team selected, system configured, training materials ready, announcement sent.

---

### Phase 2: Pilot & Prove (Test with Small Group)

**Goal:** Test the system with a small group, find and fix issues, build confidence, create success stories.

**Launch Pilot:**
- [ ] Train pilot users with hands-on, real-scenario sessions
- [ ] **Champions lead their teams, super-users support peers**
- [ ] Go live with pilot projects (**keep group small to control blast radius**)
- [ ] Run real work through system (actual suppliers, actual orders, actual approvals)
- [ ] No fake data - if it can't handle real work now, it won't later

**Daily Support & Monitoring:**
- [ ] Daily check-ins with pilot team (what worked? what didn't?)
- [ ] **Watch for early resisters - address negativity immediately** (one-on-ones, not public confrontation)
- [ ] Champions available for drop-in support
- [ ] Super-users helping their immediate peers
- [ ] Track pilot metrics (logins, transactions, questions)

**Issue Detection & Response:**
- [ ] Collect honest feedback daily (create safe space for criticism)
- [ ] Fix quick wins immediately (show you're listening)
- [ ] Document harder issues for refinement phase
- [ ] **Easier to spot workflow problems with small group** (this is why you stay small)

**Success Documentation:**
- [ ] Document early wins (even small ones)
- [ ] Capture success stories ("This saved me 2 hours today")
- [ ] Get testimonial quotes from pilot users
- [ ] Track time savings, error reduction, approval speed

**Refinement Planning:**
- [ ] Identify common questions (these become FAQ)
- [ ] Note confusing features (need clearer training or UI fixes)
- [ ] List workflow adjustments needed
- [ ] Update training materials based on real questions

**Pilot Sign-Off:**
- [ ] Get pilot team's buy-in for wider rollout ("Would you recommend this?")
- [ ] **Pilot champions and super-users agree to train next wave**
- [ ] Celebrate pilot success (even if it was messy)

**Phase 2 Complete When:** Pilot ran real work for 2-4 weeks, issues identified, pilot team ready to champion wider rollout.

---

### Phase 3: Rollout (Company-Wide Adoption)

**Goal:** Roll out team-by-team, leveraging pilot champions, building momentum, addressing resistance.

**Refinement First:**
- [ ] Fix issues identified in pilot
- [ ] Update training materials (FAQs, additional screenshots)
- [ ] Simplify confusing workflows
- [ ] Prepare success stories from pilot

**Team-by-Team Rollout:**
- [ ] **Roll out one team at a time** (NOT everyone at once - control blast radius)
- [ ] Week 1: Team A, Week 2: Team B, etc.
- [ ] Train each team with hands-on sessions using their real workflows
- [ ] **Pilot champions and super-users train new teams** (peer-to-peer more credible)
- [ ] Each team has embedded super-users as first point of contact

**Communication During Rollout:**
- [ ] **Remind teams WHY they're doing this** (refer back to their documented frustrations)
- [ ] Share pilot success stories
- [ ] Share progress factually (celebrate metrics, not theatrically)
- [ ] Provide ongoing tips and shortcuts

**Active Support:**
- [ ] Daily office hours for first week of each team's rollout
- [ ] Champions visibly using system and sharing experience
- [ ] Super-users helping peers in real-time
- [ ] Quick response to questions (don't let frustration build)

**Monitoring & Intervention:**
- [ ] Track adoption metrics weekly (logins, usage, transactions)
- [ ] **Address vocal resisters immediately** (one-on-ones, extra support)
- [ ] Address quiet non-adopters (they're struggling, not refusing)
- [ ] Provide additional training where needed

**Parallel Running:**
- [ ] Run old and new systems in parallel temporarily (safety net)
- [ ] Set and communicate hard cutoff date for old system
- [ ] Repeat cutoff date weekly: "3 weeks until Excel POs end"
- [ ] No exceptions to cutoff (cultural message matters)

**Leadership Visibility:**
- [ ] MD/Directors using system publicly
- [ ] Leaders approving via system only (no email exceptions)
- [ ] No micro-frustrations from senior staff
- [ ] Champions sharing their usage professionally

**Phase 3 Complete When:** All teams trained, 85%+ actively using system, old system ready to sunset.

---

### Phase 4: Sunset & Optimize (Lock It In)

**Goal:** Retire old system, address final resisters, optimize workflows, establish continuous improvement.

**Sunset Old System:**
- [ ] Cutoff date arrives - old system becomes view-only or shut down
- [ ] No exceptions - new system is the only way forward
- [ ] Provide support for final stragglers (help, don't shame)
- [ ] Communicate that dual systems are over

**Final Resistance Management:**
- [ ] **Identify persistent non-users** (data shows who's not engaged)
- [ ] One-on-one conversations: intensive support, clear expectations
- [ ] **Final decision on vocal toxic resisters** (nuclear option if needed: change the person)
- [ ] Document attempts to support before any personnel decisions

**Measure Success:**
- [ ] Calculate full adoption rate (target: 85%+)
- [ ] Measure time savings (compare to pre-implementation baseline)
- [ ] Measure error reduction (invoice errors, duplicate payments, etc.)
- [ ] Measure process speed (approval time, order-to-delivery, etc.)
- [ ] Validate ROI vs. business case promises

**Recognition:**
- [ ] **Privately recognize champions and super-users** (they made this happen)
- [ ] Share final metrics factually
- [ ] Thank the team for pushing through the learning curve

**Optimization:**
- [ ] Gather feedback on what could be better
- [ ] Identify power users for advanced training
- [ ] Optimize workflows based on usage patterns
- [ ] Configure additional features now that core adoption is solid

**Continuous Improvement:**
- [ ] Monthly check-ins with champions
- [ ] Quarterly review of adoption metrics
- [ ] Ongoing feedback loop
- [ ] Plan for new feature rollouts
- [ ] Document lessons learned for next implementation

**Phase 4 Complete When:** Old system retired, 85%+ adoption achieved, continuous improvement process established.

The £50k mistake isn't buying the wrong software. It's buying the right software and implementing it wrong.

### What You've Learned

**The five types of resistance** and why they're rational responses to change:
- Capacity: They genuinely don't have time for the learning curve
- Status quo: The old way works for them because they can't see the invisible waste
- Skepticism: They've been burned before and need proof, not promises
- Confidence: They're afraid of looking incompetent, not actually incapable
- Ownership: They weren't consulted and feel disrespected by the process

**The five pillars** that drive successful adoption:
- Get buy-in before you buy and identify champions early
- Win hearts and minds by solving their problems, not company problems
- Make training practical with real work, not theoretical feature tours
- Start small to control blast radius and prove it works
- Track progress, share it factually, address resistance directly

- Executive sponsorship matters more than you think (including micro-frustrations)

- Hard cutoffs are non-negotiable
- Some people won't change, and that's a personnel decision

**The checklist** that turns theory into execution across four clear phases.

### Template 3: One-on-One Conversation Script (For Non-Adopters)

*Use this when data shows someone isn't using the system*

---

**Context:** You've noticed [Name] hasn't logged into the system for 2 weeks, or is still using the old way.

**Approach:** Private conversation (not public), curious not accusatory.

---

**Opening:**
"Hey [Name], got a minute? Wanted to check in about [software]. I noticed you haven't been using it much - what's going on?"

**Listen first.** Let them talk. Common responses and how to handle:

---

**Response 1: "I'm too busy"**

**Your reply:**
"I get it - you're slammed. That's actually why we implemented this - to save you time. Can we do a quick 10-minute session right now using one of your actual POs? I'll sit with you and we'll do it together."

**Action:** Hands-on session immediately, with their real work.

---

**Response 2: "I don't understand it / It's confusing"**

**Your reply:**
"Totally normal - it's new. Lots of people found it confusing at first. Let me pair you with [super-user name] who's using it successfully. They can show you the shortcuts."

**Action:** Buddy system with successful peer user.

---

**Response 3: "The old way is faster for me"**

**Your reply:**
"Right now, yes - because you're an Excel expert. But I'd love to track your time this week. I think you're spending more time than you realize on [specific task]. Mind if we test it?"

**Action:** Time tracking exercise to make invisible time waste visible.

---

**Response 4: "It doesn't work for my workflow"**

**Your reply:**
"Show me your workflow. Let's see if we can configure [software] to match it, or if there's genuinely a gap we need to fix."

**Action:** Workflow mapping session. Either configure to fit, or identify legitimate product gap.

---

**Response 5: Resistance / "I don't want to"**

**Your reply:**
"I hear you. But here's where we are: the old system is getting shut down on [date]. This is how we're working now. It's not optional. What support do you need to get there?"

**Action:** Clear expectation + offer of support. Not punishment, but accountability.

---

**Closing:**
"Here's what I'm committing to: [specific support you'll provide]. What I need from you: [specific action - use it for next 5 POs, attend refresher training, etc.]. Let's check in again in 1 week. Sound fair?"

**Follow-up:** Calendar reminder for 1-week check-in. Track if they engage.

---

**If after intensive support they still refuse:**
See Chapter 3, Pitfall 8 (the nuclear option). Some people won't change, and that's a personnel decision.
