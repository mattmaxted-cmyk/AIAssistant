Introduction

Why commercial control breaks down

Most commercial teams at UK main contractors are competent. The people are skilled, the intentions are right and the knowledge of the contracts is there. The problem is not skill or effort — it is that the processes used to manage commercial workloads were designed for a smaller, simpler operation and have never been systematically updated as volumes grew.

Payment applications are managed in spreadsheets, pay less notice deadlines are tracked in personal calendar reminders, variations are agreed on site and chased up weeks later, and approval chains run through email threads and phone calls with no single record of what was decided and when. Disputes escalate because the documentation to support the claim simply does not exist in a usable form, not because the entitlement was absent.

A contractor managing five subcontract packages can run this manually without significant risk. One managing twenty-five cannot, not reliably, and not under the time pressures of live projects where something urgent is always competing for attention. The failure point is not any individual decision. It is the accumulation of small process gaps that, over a project lifecycle, translate directly into lost margin.

The result is margin leakage that is entirely preventable: variations that were valid but went unrecovered because notification was late, applications that were over-assessed because the assessment process was rushed, pay less notices that were missed because no one was monitoring the deadline across thirty live packages simultaneously, and disputes that were lost not on merit but because the paperwork to support the position was never properly maintained.

This guide is structured around the three areas where commercial control most commonly fails in practice: payments, variations and approvals. Each chapter sets out what good practice looks like, where the common failures occur, and what you can do to reduce your exposure. It is written for commercial teams doing the work, not for executives looking at the problem from a distance.

2,100+

The payment application cycle is the commercial heartbeat of a construction project. Get it right and cash flow is predictable, disputes are rare and the supply chain stays engaged. Get it wrong and even a profitable project can put a business under financial pressure.

For main contractors, the application cycle runs in both directions. Upstream, you are submitting applications to the client. Downstream, you are receiving and assessing applications from subcontractors. The commercial risk sits in both directions, but the downstream process is where most margin leakage occurs.

What a well-run payment application process looks like

1
Subcontract setup with clear payment terms
Assessment dates, submission deadlines and payment due dates are defined in the subcontract and recorded centrally. Everyone knows the dates before work starts.
2
Application received and logged
The application is received, date-stamped and logged against the correct subcontract package. The assessment clock starts from the correct date, not from when someone got round to looking at it.
3
Assessment conducted against the subcontract
Each line of the application is assessed against the subcontract, the programme, the agreed scope and any approved variations. The assessment is documented with supporting rationale.
4
Pay less notice issued if required
If the assessed value is less than the applied sum, a valid pay less notice is issued within the prescribed period. The notice includes the sum considered due and the basis for the reduction.
5
Payment made on the due date
Payment is made on the contractual due date. Late payment without a valid notice exposes you to statutory interest claims and potential adjudication.

Most commercial teams understand this process in theory. The failure point is almost always step 2 or step 4. Applications arrive by email, get missed or sit in someone's inbox. Assessment deadlines are not tracked. Pay less notice windows are forgotten because no one is monitoring the dates systematically across 20 or 30 live packages.

What to do about it

Map out every live subcontract package and identify the assessment date and pay less notice deadline for the current application cycle. If you cannot do this in under 10 minutes, your tracking is not tight enough. The test of a good system is whether a new team member could pick up any package and immediately see the current status and the next critical date.

Final account settlement is where the commercial outcome of a project is determined. It is also where the gap between what was earned and what is recovered tends to be largest, and where contractors with strong contemporaneous records consistently outperform those relying on end-of-project reconstruction.

The dynamics of final account negotiation are well understood by experienced commercial teams: the client's QS will challenge every claim that lacks supporting documentation, use programme delays as leverage to offset variation entitlements, and apply pressure through the threat of a protracted settlement process. The contractors who navigate this successfully are not the ones with the most aggressive commercial team. They are the ones who have been building their final account position throughout the project rather than assembling it at the end.

Where final account recovery is lost

Claims submitted without contemporaneous records. The client's QS gains significant negotiating leverage the moment a claim cannot be supported with records from the time the instruction was given or the additional cost was incurred. Retrospective valuations, reconstructed programmes and assembled correspondence are all weaker than records that existed at the time and were maintained as a matter of course.

Variation claims presented in bulk at the end of the project. A list of variations submitted at final account invites a challenge to every item simultaneously. The same claims presented and agreed progressively throughout the project, with each variation valued and signed off as it arises, are dramatically harder to dispute at the end because the client has already acknowledged each one individually.

Loss and expense claims that are difficult to substantiate. Loss and expense is the category where the largest sums are most frequently under-recovered. The entitlement is often real and the cause clearly the client's risk. The problem is that demonstrating the actual financial impact requires detailed cost records at a level that most commercial teams do not maintain during delivery, making the claim difficult to quantify with the precision that survives scrutiny at settlement.

📋
Poor commercial management destroys between 3% and 7% of project value on average across the industry, representing £15 million to £35 million in annual value leakage for a £500 million turnover business. The majority of this is recoverable through better contract administration practice, not through changing the contracts themselves.
What to do about it

Treat final account preparation as a project-long activity rather than a post-completion task. The variation register, the assessed application history, the correspondence record and the cost allocation data should all be in a state that could support a final account submission at any point during the project. If a settlement discussion started tomorrow, how long would it take your team to assemble the supporting documentation? That answer tells you where the gap is.

Cash flow forecasting in construction is inherently complex, and experienced commercial directors know that the gap between a forecast and the actual cash position is rarely caused by a single large error. It is almost always the cumulative effect of several small divergences that each seemed manageable in isolation but compound over the duration of a project.

The most persistent causes are also the least dramatic: variations that are on the register but not yet valued, retentions that are larger than the forecast assumed because interim certificates came in lower than projected, applications assessed at a lower figure than submitted without the difference being reflected in the forecast, and VAT timing that pulls cash out earlier than the revenue model anticipated. None of these are unusual. They are the normal conditions of a live construction project, and they explain why even well-run commercial teams find their actual cash position diverging from the forecast by the middle of a project.

The three data streams that drive forecast accuracy

Accurate cash flow forecasting in construction requires three data streams to be current and connected. The first is project-level cost data: committed costs, variations approved and pending, retention positions and current assessment values. The second is cash timing: not the invoice value but the actual date the cash is expected to move, accounting for contractual payment periods, historical client payment behaviour and any disputes in progress. The third is pipeline: the forward view of anticipated application values and expected assessment outcomes over the next three to six months.

Most commercial teams have all three of these data sources available. The forecast accuracy problem is that they are maintained separately, updated at different frequencies and reconciled at month end rather than in real time. By the time the monthly report is produced, the data feeding it is already two to three weeks old, and the decisions being made on the basis of it are being made against a position that has already moved.

What to do about it

Build scenario variants into your cash flow model rather than maintaining a single forecast line. A base case, a delayed payment scenario (shifting client payments from 30 to 45 days) and a variations-delayed scenario (pushing unvalued variations back one cycle) will give you a realistic range and reduce the impact of any single assumption being wrong. The goal is not a perfect forecast. It is a forecast that remains useful even when individual assumptions prove incorrect, which in construction they always do.

2c

Subcontractor Over-claiming: Building an Assessment Process That Catches It

Exaggerated claims were identified as one of the leading causes of construction disputes in the 2024 King's College London adjudication report. The scale of over-claiming in subcontractor applications varies widely by package type and subcontractor, but the commercial teams most exposed are those whose assessment process does not have the structure to identify and challenge it systematically across a large portfolio of live packages.

Over-claiming takes several forms, and they are not all deliberate. Some subcontractors apply for more than they have earned because their own internal cost tracking is poor and they are applying on the basis of estimated rather than measured progress. Others apply at the upper end of a reasonable range, knowing that a thorough assessment will bring the figure down but that an understaffed or time-pressured commercial team may not have the capacity to challenge every line. A smaller proportion are making claims they know to be unsupportable, relying on the paying party's failure to issue a valid notice in time.

The patterns that indicate systematic over-claiming

Applications that increase disproportionately relative to programme progress. A subcontractor's application value should broadly track their percentage of work complete. When applications consistently run ahead of assessed progress by more than a reasonable tolerance, particularly in the early stages of a package when the tendency is to front-load claims, this is the primary indicator to investigate.

Variation claims submitted in bulk without supporting calculation. A subcontractor that submits a lump sum variation claim with no supporting breakdown is making it structurally difficult to assess. This is sometimes deliberate. A well-run assessment process requires breakdowns as a condition of assessment, which shifts the burden back to the subcontractor to justify the figure before it can be considered.

Daywork sheets submitted retrospectively without contemporaneous records. Daywork is one of the most commonly abused mechanisms in subcontract administration. Sheets submitted weeks after the work was carried out, without signatures from the site manager at the time, are almost impossible to verify and easy to inflate. The discipline required is contemporaneous sign-off at the time of the work, not retrospective agreement at valuation.

What to do about it

Establish a consistent assessment standard across all packages and apply it without exception. Every application should be assessed against the subcontract, the programme and the agreed scope. Every reduction from the applied sum should be documented with a clear rationale that could be defended in adjudication. The commercial teams that do this consistently find that over-claiming reduces over time, because subcontractors adjust their applications to what they know will be assessed rather than what they hope will be accepted.

Variations: How Contractors Lose Money They Were Entitled To

10 to 20%
Typical variation value as a percentage of the final account on UK projects
No. 1
Reason contractors fail to recover variation costs: late or absent notification
6%
Margin difference between projects where variations are well-managed versus poorly managed

Variations typically represent 10 to 20% of the final account on most UK construction projects. Sometimes significantly more. The difference between a project finishing at 8% margin and 2% margin often comes down entirely to how well variations were managed, not to the original contract price or the quality of the build.

The money is there and the legal entitlement is real. The problem is the process that is supposed to capture it, and specifically the gap between the work being done and the claim being made in a form that is timely, documented and defensible.

Where variation recovery fails

Late notification is the single biggest reason contractors fail to recover variation costs. Work is instructed, carried out and completed, and the claim is raised weeks or months later at a point where the client's QS can legitimately ask why notice was not given at the time. Negotiating leverage disappears and settlements are reached for less than the full entitlement because the contemporaneous record to support the full claim does not exist.

Informal instructions acted upon without formal records create a different but equally damaging problem. An architect requests a change on site, a verbal instruction is given and the work is done. When the contractor raises the variation at final account, the client disputes whether the instruction was ever formally given. Without a written record from the time, the contractor carries the burden of proof and has no contemporaneous evidence to support the position.

Scope creep absorbed without valuation is a slower-moving version of the same issue. Small changes accumulate over a project, each of which seems too minor to raise formally at the time. Cumulatively they can represent significant cost, but by final account they are difficult to isolate and value, and the client has no expectation of paying for them because they were never flagged during the project.

💷
On a £5 million project, variations at 15% of final account represent £750,000. If 20% of that is unrecovered due to poor notification and documentation, that is £150,000 of margin lost that the contractor was legally entitled to. Not lost to bad pricing or poor delivery. Lost to process.

Variation management checklist

Every instruction that differs from the contract scope is identified and recorded at the time it is given
A formal variation notice or early warning is issued within the contractual timeframe, not retrospectively
Each variation is valued as it is agreed, not left to final account
Site-level changes are communicated to the commercial team on the same day, not at the next valuation
A live variation register is maintained for every project, accessible to both the site and commercial team
Subcontractor variation claims are assessed within the contractual assessment period and responded to formally
What to do about it

Look at your last three completed projects. Pull the final account variation register. For each unrecovered or under-recovered variation, identify the reason. In most cases it will be one of three things: late notification, no written instruction, or a claim raised too late to be assessed properly. The pattern will tell you where your process needs strengthening.

Approval bottlenecks are rarely caused by people being uncooperative or difficult. They are caused by processes that route too many decisions through the same senior individual with no visibility of what is waiting, no delegation framework that allows lower-value decisions to be handled at an appropriate level, and no escalation route for when that person is on site, travelling or simply overwhelmed by competing priorities.

The consequences are felt across the entire business operation. Procurement decisions are delayed, which in turn delays site delivery and can push programmes out. Subcontractor payments are held up, which damages supply chain relationships and, when delayed beyond the contractual due date, creates statutory interest liability that accumulates silently until it becomes a formal claim. Meanwhile, internal teams spend significant time chasing approvals by email and phone rather than applying that time to work that actually moves the project forward.

The characteristics of a good approval framework

1
Value-based routing
Low-value approvals are handled by the appropriate level without needing director sign-off. Clear thresholds are defined and communicated. A £500 purchase order does not need the same approval chain as a £500,000 subcontract order.
2
Delegation and cover
When an approver is unavailable, there is a defined fallback. Approvals do not sit in an inbox while someone is on site, travelling or on leave. The business does not stop because one person is temporarily unavailable.
3
Visibility of what is pending
Approvers can see everything waiting for them, ordered by urgency. Requestors can see the status of their submission without having to chase. No one is surprised by a delay they did not know was happening.
4
Escalation rules
If an approval has not been actioned within a defined period, it is automatically escalated. Time-sensitive approvals (such as those with contractual deadlines attached) are flagged explicitly.

Most commercial teams do not have all four of these elements formally defined. Many have none of them. The result is an informal approval system that works adequately when the business is quiet and workloads are light, but fails visibly when it is under pressure, which is precisely the condition under which good commercial process matters most.

What to do about it

Map the approval chain for a standard procurement request from first submission to final approval. Count the steps, the people involved and the average time at each stage. Then ask: what happens when the person at step 3 is on site? How is that currently handled? In most businesses, the honest answer is "informally." That is where the risk sits.

The Audit Trail: Why Documentation Wins or Loses Disputes

⚖️
Adjudication referrals in UK construction increased 9% year on year in 2024, reaching 2,264 referrals. The most common claim type was technical payment claims (63%) where the dispute was not about the value of the work but about whether the correct notices were issued at the correct time. Adjudication costs typically range from £20,000 to £50,000.

In a construction dispute, the party with the better documentation almost always wins, and that is not the party that did better work or that has the stronger technical argument. It is the party with the contemporaneous records that demonstrate what happened, when it happened and who was responsible for each decision. The quality of the work and the strength of the entitlement are secondary if the documentation to support them is absent or inadequate.

This is not because the legal system is unfair. It is because in the absence of contemporaneous documentation, adjudicators and courts cannot determine what actually happened from conflicting accounts alone. They default to what can be proved. If you cannot demonstrate when a notice was issued, when an instruction was given or when an assessment was completed, the other party's version of events carries more weight than it should, and the outcome reflects the strength of the paperwork rather than the underlying merits of the case.

What a complete commercial audit trail looks like

Every subcontract application received, date-stamped, with a record of when and how it was received
Every assessment documented with the values applied and the basis for any deduction
Every pay less notice with a record of the date issued and the method of delivery
Every variation instruction recorded with the date, the instructing party and the agreed scope
Every approval with a record of who approved it, when and on what basis
Every payment with a record of what was paid, when and against which application

This level of documentation is entirely achievable and does not require exceptional effort when it is built into the normal commercial process from the outset. The failure happens when documentation is treated as an afterthought, something to be compiled retrospectively if a dispute arises rather than something that is maintained as a matter of course during the normal administration of each package. By the time a dispute arises it is too late to create contemporaneous records, and the value of maintaining them properly is entirely front-loaded in the way that good commercial process always is.

The cost of a dispute

A construction adjudication typically costs between £20,000 and £50,000 in professional fees alone, before considering management time. A claim valued at £150,000 that settles for £90,000 due to inadequate documentation has effectively cost the business £110,000, including the professional fees. The same claim with a complete audit trail would likely have settled at or close to the full value.

What to do about it

Pick a live project and run a documentation audit. For the last three application cycles, can you produce: the date the application was received, the date the assessment was completed, the date the pay less notice was issued (if applicable) and the method of delivery? If any of these cannot be produced quickly and clearly, that is the gap to close before a dispute makes it consequential.

Summary

Building a commercial control framework that actually works

The five areas in this guide (payments, pay less notices, variations, approvals and documentation) are not independent problems. They are connected parts of the same commercial process. A weakness in one creates exposure in all the others. A pay less notice missed because the assessment was delayed. A variation unrecovered because the approval that should have captured it was stuck in someone's inbox. A dispute lost because the documentation that would have supported the claim was never systematically maintained.

Good commercial control does not require exceptional effort or expensive systems. It requires clear processes, defined responsibilities, visibility of critical dates and a culture where documentation is treated as part of the work, not an addition to it.

The businesses with strong commercial control are not necessarily the ones with the most experienced teams or the highest billing rates. They are the ones with the tightest processes. Processes that work when people are busy, when cover is needed, when volume increases and when disputes arise. Those are the conditions that test commercial control, and they are the conditions under which most businesses are currently operating.

Start with the area of greatest current exposure. Use the checklists and audit prompts in this guide to identify where your process is weakest. Close one gap at a time. The cumulative effect of incremental improvements to commercial control is, over a full project lifecycle, significant.
