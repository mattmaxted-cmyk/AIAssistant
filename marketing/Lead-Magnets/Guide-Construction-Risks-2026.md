# Construction in 2026: The Risks UK Contractors Can't Afford to Ignore

**A practical guide for MDs, FDs and Commercial Directors at UK main contractors**

---

## Introduction

Running a construction business in the UK has never been straightforward. But 2026 brings a specific combination of pressures that, taken together, represent one of the most challenging operating environments the industry has faced in years.

Costs are rising. Legislation is changing. The talent pipeline is broken. And the businesses that fail to adapt their processes and systems are the ones most exposed.

This guide covers the six biggest risks facing UK contractors in 2026 — what they are, why they matter, and what you can do about them.

---

## Risk 1: Rising Costs Are Compressing Margins on Fixed-Price Contracts

Construction costs are forecast to rise by approximately 3.6% in 2026. Tender prices are tracking at around 3.45%. On top of that, the April 2026 National Insurance increase and further minimum wage rises are adding direct cost pressure to every business that employs people directly.

For contractors running fixed-price or lump-sum contracts, this creates a specific problem: the contract was priced before these increases came through. The work is already committed. The margin is already thin.

The businesses most exposed are those without robust variation management and cost forecasting — those relying on spreadsheets to track committed costs, running CVRs manually each month, and finding out about margin erosion too late to do anything about it.

**What this means in practice:** Every contractor needs tighter commercial controls in 2026. You need to know your margin position in real time, not at month end. Variations need to be captured the moment they happen, not reconstructed at final account.

---

## Risk 2: New Legislation Is Coming — And Most Contractors Aren't Ready

Several significant legislative changes take effect in 2026. Each carries its own compliance burden.

**Building Safety Levy (1 October 2026)**
A new levy on residential developments above 10 dwellings comes into force on 1 October 2026. It has been designed to raise approximately £3.4bn over 10 years to fund remediation of dangerous buildings. If you work in residential, this affects your project economics from Q4 2026.

**Building Regulations — Part B (30 September 2026)**
Amendments to Part B require second staircases in residential buildings over 18 metres. Projects already in design need to be reviewed. Those in delivery need to understand the contractual implications.

**Energy Performance Certificates (October 2026)**
Major reforms to EPC requirements and new skills requirements for energy installations take effect in October 2026. If you are working on projects with an energy or retrofit component, your supply chain needs to be compliant.

**Procurement Act 2023 — Now Live**
This came into force in February 2025 and reshapes public sector procurement with centralised digital platforms and stricter transparency requirements. If public sector work is part of your pipeline, your commercial team needs to understand the new rules around tender evaluation and contract award.

**What this means in practice:** Compliance risk is not just a legal problem — it is a commercial one. Failing to price legislative changes into contracts, or failing to track compliance obligations across your supply chain, exposes you to cost overruns and disputes.

---

## Risk 3: The Industry's Insolvency Rate Remains at Crisis Levels

Construction accounted for 17.1% of all company insolvencies in England and Wales in January 2026. Over the 12 months to January 2026, 3,912 construction firms became insolvent. That is 21.5% above pre-pandemic levels. Construction has had the highest insolvency rate of any UK sector for the fourth consecutive year.

The direct risk to your business is not just your own solvency — it is the solvency of the businesses you depend on. Subcontractors going under mid-project. Suppliers unable to fulfil orders. Main contractors collapsing and leaving unpaid applications behind them.

The businesses that manage this risk are the ones with visibility. They know which subcontractors are financially stressed before they fail. They know which applications are outstanding. They have the audit trail to make claims if something goes wrong.

**What this means in practice:** Subcontractor financial risk needs to be monitored, not assumed. Your payment application process needs to be watertight — every application tracked, every notice issued on time, every payment decision documented.

---

## Risk 4: The Talent Pipeline Is Breaking Down

Over 140,000 construction job vacancies remain unfilled across the UK. Construction employment fell to its lowest level in nearly 25 years in late 2025 — yet skilled roles, supervisory positions and commercial staff remain in acute shortage.

For main contractors, the talent problem shows up in two places.

First, on site — skilled operatives are harder to find and more expensive to retain. Labour cost inflation is outpacing what was priced into contracts.

Second, in the commercial office — experienced QSs, commercial managers and contracts managers are in short supply. When you do find them, they are spending significant time on tasks that should not require their level of seniority: chasing applications, processing paperwork, reconstructing cost data from spreadsheets.

The businesses that retain commercial talent are the ones that remove the administrative burden. Nobody with 15 years of QS experience stays in a role where they spend three days a month manually processing payment applications.

**What this means in practice:** Retention and productivity are connected. Streamlining commercial workflows does not just save cost — it makes the role more attractive and frees your best people to do the work that actually protects margin.

---

## Risk 5: Poor Internal Processes Are Costing More Than You Think

This is not a new risk, but it is the one that quietly compounds everything else.

Fragmented systems. Payment applications managed on spreadsheets. Pay less notice deadlines tracked in calendars. Variations agreed on site and never formally captured. Invoices processed manually and matched against paper delivery notes.

These are not edge cases. They describe the operating model of the majority of UK main contractors right now.

The cost is real. A contractor processing 500 purchase orders a month, with 15 minutes of average handling time per order, is spending 125 hours a month on procurement admin alone. At a blended cost of £25 per hour, that is over £37,000 a year. Just in admin time. Before you count the errors, the disputes and the payment deadlines that slip.

When a pay less notice deadline is missed, the full application amount becomes due. No deductions. No comeback. One missed deadline on a large application can cost more than a year of software subscription.

**What this means in practice:** Process inefficiency has a direct financial cost. The businesses that quantify this cost and address it systematically are the ones that protect margin when everything else is getting tighter.

---

## Risk 6: Competitors Are Starting to Adopt Technology — and the Gap Is Widening

Only 12% of UK construction firms have deployed technology meaningfully in their operations. 88% are running on legacy processes or fragmented point solutions. For now, this means most businesses are equally exposed. But that is changing.

The contractors that automate procurement, digitise payment applications and integrate their commercial and operational data are starting to see the results: faster approvals, fewer disputes, better margin visibility, leaner teams.

When those businesses are tendering against you, they can operate at lower overhead. They can price more accurately. They can manage more work with the same headcount.

The risk of not adopting technology in 2026 is no longer theoretical. It is competitive.

**What this means in practice:** The question is no longer whether to modernise your systems — it is how quickly you can do it before the gap becomes a competitive disadvantage.

---

## What Connects All Six Risks

Every risk in this guide — cost pressure, legislation, insolvency, talent, process, technology — is made worse by fragmented, manual operations.

Contractors with connected systems and real-time commercial data are better placed to manage all of them. They catch margin erosion early. They track compliance obligations across their supply chain. They process payment applications accurately and on time. They retain commercial staff because the work is less administrative.

The businesses that struggle in 2026 will be the ones that try to manage increasing complexity with the same manual processes they used five years ago.
