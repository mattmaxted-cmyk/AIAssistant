Every month, across every live subcontract, the same cycle repeats.

The subcontractor submits a payment application. Your QS assesses it and issues a payment certificate. A payment notice goes out confirming what will be paid and when.

Three documents. Every subcontract. Every month.

On a spreadsheet, each one is a separate manual action. The application comes in by email. The QS opens the spreadsheet, works through it, produces a certificate. The payment notice gets drafted separately. No connection between them. No audit trail linking application to certificate to notice. Nothing flagging when a step has been missed.

Across 100 subcontractors on 10 live projects, that is closer to 800 documents a month. All manually produced. All manually tracked. All manually chased.

The errors are not the exception. They are what happens when a process this complex runs on a tool this basic.

How is your team currently managing this?

Most subcontractor over-claiming does not get flagged as a problem. It gets processed as an approved payment.

The application comes in. The commercial team is stretched. There is no structured assessment workflow. The QS certifies broadly what was submitted and moves on.

The spreadsheet has no mechanism to surface a discrepancy. No comparison against what was ordered. No check against prior certifications. Just a number in, a number out.

Individually, over-claims tend to be modest enough not to raise a flag. But run the numbers.

3% over-claim on applications across 100 subcontractors on a £30m turnover is in the region of £900,000 a year. Leaving the business. Unchallenged. Because the tool being used has no way to catch it.

I am not suggesting subcontractors are dishonest. Most of it is simply unchallenged because the process does not make it easy to challenge.

Does your team have a structured way to assess applications line by line, or is it broadly certified against what was submitted?

### Week 2 — Mon — Michael — Feature/Solution

<!-- Target: Commercial Leader -->
<!-- Objective: Product awareness -->
<!-- Type: Feature/solution — problem then solution -->
<!-- CTA: See how it works → website -->

The variation happened. Everyone on site knew it happened.

But it was agreed verbally. The QS noted it in a meeting. Someone intended to raise it formally. It never made it into the spreadsheet. By the time final account came around, there was nothing to stand it up with.

This is the part spreadsheets cannot do. They cannot capture a variation at the moment it is agreed. They cannot connect it to the contract. They cannot require both parties to confirm scope and value before it is applied.

They rely on someone remembering to add a row. And in a busy commercial team running multiple live projects, rows get missed.

StoneRise Commercial makes variation capture part of the contract workflow. Every instruction is logged, scoped, and put through dual approval before it is applied. The record exists from the moment the variation is agreed, not reconstructed from memory at final account.

By the time you get to final account, there is nothing to contest.
