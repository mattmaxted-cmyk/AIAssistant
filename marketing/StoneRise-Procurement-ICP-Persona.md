## Persona: "Chris" — The Procurement App Buyer

Chris is the procurement-focused equivalent of David. Where David feels the chaos of the payment application cycle, Chris feels the chaos of managing procurement across multiple live construction projects — on a spreadsheet that was never designed for it.

## Current Setup

The vast majority of UK construction contractors — including contractors well above £20m turnover — are still managing their entire procurement operation on spreadsheets. A quote comparison spreadsheet. A PO log spreadsheet. A supplier list spreadsheet. A delivery tracking spreadsheet. None of them connected. Chris is almost certainly one of them.

This is not a technology laggard problem. It is a market structure problem: the construction procurement software that exists either costs too much, doesn't understand construction workflows, or was designed for manufacturing. Chris has learned to make Excel work. He has templates for RFQs, a supplier tab, a PO numbering system he built himself. It functions — until it doesn't. And the point at which it stops functioning is always invisible until something has already gone wrong.

Copy can speak directly to this reality without qualification.

---

## Root Cause — The Spreadsheet Problem

Chris's pain points are not separate issues. They are all symptoms of the same root cause: running a live procurement operation on spreadsheets that were never designed for it.

The spreadsheet has no enforcement mechanism. It cannot send an RFQ to a supplier and chase the response automatically. It cannot flag that a purchase order is sitting unapproved. It does not know whether a delivery was received in full or whether the invoice matches what was ordered. It relies entirely on Chris — and his team — remembering to do the right thing at the right time. And it has no way of connecting the four key documents in any procurement transaction — the request, the quote, the purchase order, the invoice — into a single auditable chain.

At 200+ POs a month, across multiple live projects, with invoices arriving on their own schedule from dozens of suppliers — the gap between what should have been caught and what actually was caught is always there. Chris does not necessarily experience this as "a spreadsheet problem." He experiences it as a series of recurring failures: the quote that never came back, the order that went out without approval, the invoice that got paid twice, the supplier whose insurance expired without anyone knowing. The job of marketing copy is to surface the pattern he recognises and name the cause for him.

## Pain Points (Priority Order — all driven by spreadsheet dependency)

### 1. The entire RFQ process is manual — and nothing is tracked

Every request for quotation starts with Chris or someone on his team filling in a spreadsheet, copy-pasting supplier email addresses, sending individual emails, and then waiting. When quotes come back — each in a different format, some as attachments, some as figures in the body of an email — someone manually transfers the numbers into a comparison spreadsheet. There is no master view of what is outstanding. No automatic chase. No flag when a supplier hasn't responded and a site delivery is at risk. The only way Chris knows a quote is overdue is when a site manager rings to find out where the materials are.

Across a contractor running 8–10 live projects, there might be 30–50 RFQs active at any time. Every single one of them is being tracked — or not tracked — on a spreadsheet.

This is the primary campaign focus.

### 2. Approvals happen outside the spreadsheet — which means they don't really happen

There is no approval workflow in a spreadsheet. The best most contractors manage is an email to a manager, a WhatsApp message, or a verbal sign-off in the office. None of this is enforced. There is no mechanism to stop an order going out before approval is confirmed. There is no escalation path if the approver is on site and not checking email. High-value commitments get waved through because the chase is awkward and the site is waiting. When something goes wrong — an over-budget order, a duplicate purchase, a cost that shouldn't have been committed — nobody can reconstruct the approval chain with confidence because it was never captured in the first place.

### 3. Invoices don't match POs and the spreadsheet can't catch it

A supplier sends an invoice. Someone in finance tries to cross-reference it against the PO log — a spreadsheet. The line description may not match. The quantity may be slightly different. The project code may be missing. Without a direct link between the invoice and the original PO, delivery record, and approved quote, every discrepancy has to be caught manually by someone who knows enough to spot it. Most aren't caught. They get paid because querying an invoice takes longer than approving it, and nobody has a systematic way to flag the problem. Chris knows he's paying for things he shouldn't be. He doesn't know the number — because the spreadsheet has never been able to tell him.

### 4. Supplier information lives across spreadsheets, emails, and shared drives — never in one place

The preferred supplier list is a spreadsheet. Insurance certificates are in someone's inbox. Accreditation documents are on a shared drive, in a folder structure that only one person fully understands. When a certificate expires, there is no alert — just the moment someone checks and realises it ran out three months ago. When a new project starts, the buying team rebuild their supplier shortlist from habit and memory because the spreadsheet doesn't capture performance history, stop lists, or which suppliers performed well on comparable work. When a supplier dispute comes up, there is no record to reference — just recollections and emails that may or may not still be findable.

### 5. The spreadsheet produces no spend data — so there is no negotiating power

Chris cannot easily answer: how much did we spend with this supplier last year? What is the average price per tonne of concrete across our projects? Which material categories are over-running? Which suppliers consistently deliver short? The answers exist — scattered across PO logs on different project folders, on different people's computers, in formats that don't combine cleanly. Pulling a supplier spend report takes hours, if it gets done at all. Without consolidated data, every supplier negotiation starts from zero. The business has real buying power — it simply cannot demonstrate it, because a spreadsheet has never been able to aggregate across projects in real time.

## A Specific Week in Chris's Life

It is Tuesday morning. Chris has 11 unread emails from suppliers with quotes. Each one is in a different format. He opens his comparison spreadsheet and starts copying numbers across manually. A project manager needs a decision by this afternoon. He has another 6 RFQs with no responses yet — he doesn't know if they were received, or if the suppliers are just slow. He'll send a chase email. Then another one.

There are 3 invoices on his desk that finance have flagged. He thinks one might be a duplicate. He opens the PO log — a spreadsheet — and starts scrolling. The line descriptions don't quite match the invoice. He'll have to check the original email to confirm what was actually ordered.

A site manager called yesterday about a delivery that came up short — 15 sheets instead of 25. The supplier is disputing it. The delivery wasn't logged on the spreadsheet at the time. Chris has an email he sent on the day saying it was short, but nothing formally recorded. There's no signed delivery note.

His MD asked this morning what the business spent on plant hire last quarter. Chris has three project folders, each with its own PO log spreadsheet, each formatted slightly differently. He'll get to it this afternoon, if the morning clears.

None of this is a crisis. None of it is unusual. This is the job — and the spreadsheet is at the centre of all of it.

## Buying Motivation

- Replace the RFQ email process with a system that sends, chases, and collates quotes automatically — so quote comparison takes minutes, not hours
- Approval workflows that enforce sign-off before orders are placed, with a complete audit trail for every commitment
- Three-way matching that systematically catches invoice discrepancies before payment — not after
- A supplier directory with compliance documents, performance history, and spend data in one place
- Spend analytics that give Chris — and his MD — real visibility on where money is being committed

---

## What Chris Is NOT Buying

- An enterprise procurement suite that requires 6 months of implementation
- A system that was designed for manufacturing and has been "adapted" for construction
- A tool that his team will refuse to use because it's slower than email
- Software that requires a consultant to configure

He will buy something that looks like it was built for a construction site office, that his team can use from day one, and that makes the quote process visibly faster within the first week.

---

## Decision Authority

**Medium to High.** In contractors with a dedicated procurement function, Chris is often the budget holder and initiates the purchase decision. Final sign-off typically requires MD or FD approval above a certain contract value. In smaller contractors where procurement sits under Commercial or Operations, the Commercial Director or MD is the decision maker — Chris is the key influencer.

This means content must work at two levels:
- Procurement Leaders: efficiency, day-to-day relief, tool they'll actually use
- Operations/Finance Leaders: cost control, margin protection, audit trail, business case

## Key Metrics Chris Cares About

| Metric | Context |
|--------|---------|
| Time per PO (end to end) | From request to order placed — current baseline is typically 20–45 mins; should be under 10 |
| Quote response rate | What % of RFQs get responses — currently invisible |
| Approval turnaround time | How long orders sit waiting for sign-off |
| Invoice discrepancy rate | What % of invoices have errors — currently unknown for most |
| Spend under management | What % of procurement spend goes through a defined process |
| Supplier compliance rate | What % of active suppliers have current insurance/accreditations on file |

## Meta Ad Hooks

These are opening lines for Meta creatives. The only job of a hook is to make Chris stop scrolling because he recognises his own situation. No product name. No features. No benefit claims. Just a specific moment he has lived.

**What kills a hook:**
- Any form of "tired of..." or "struggling with..."
- Naming the product before he's stopped scrolling
- Listing features (RFQs, approvals, invoices)
- Generic efficiency language ("focus on strategy, not admin")
- Anything that sounds like a marketer wrote it about procurement rather than someone who does procurement

The test: could Chris have thought this himself on a Tuesday morning? If yes, it works. If it sounds like an ad, it doesn't.

Use one hook per creative. Never combine two.

## Content That Will Not Land

- Feature lists ("our system does RFQs, POs, invoices, approvals...")
- Vendor-speak ("streamline your procurement workflow with our intelligent platform")
- Generic efficiency claims ("save time and money on procurement")
- Anything that implies procurement teams are doing it wrong — they are doing the best they can with the tools they have

## Objections and How to Address Them

| Objection | Response Angle |
|-----------|---------------|
| "We've tried software before and it didn't fit construction" | Built by people who ran construction businesses. Not adapted — purpose-built. |
| "My team won't use it" | Designed to replace email and Excel, not to add complexity on top of them. Adoption is the point, not an afterthought. |
| "We're too busy to implement something new" | The busier you are, the more it costs you to stay manual. One lost invoice discrepancy at scale pays for a year's subscription. |
| "I don't have budget authority" | Arm Chris with the business case. Quantify time per PO × volume × hourly rate. Give him the number to take to the MD. |
| "We're not big enough to need this" | If you're placing 100+ POs a month, you're big enough for this to be costing you real money. |
